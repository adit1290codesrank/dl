Business shorthands expand into complex SQL — "MPY" doesn't map to a column or value, it means Marine and protective coating so in sql its SMU IN ('Protective Coating', 'Marine') AND NatureOfTransaction = 'Sales'. The user never mentions those column names or values.

Why this is completely new and better:
Standard Seq2Seq models try to generate SQL word-by-word, which is why they fail at complex expansions (they hallucinate syntax errors). Instead, our new architecture will generate an Abstract Syntax Tree (AST). The neural network only has to predict high-level "Concepts" or "Macros", and a C++ compiler will translate that tree into perfectly valid, massively complex SQL.
For example, for the query "Show me MPY sales for OBD number 1234": The neural network outputs a clean, small tree: [QUERY] -> [SELECT_ALL] [WHERE] -> [MACRO_MPY] [AND] [OBD_EQUALS_1234]
The C++ Symbolic Compiler takes this and instantly expands it to: SELECT * FROM Sales WHERE SMU IN ('Protective Coating', 'Marine') AND NatureOfTransaction = 'Sales' AND PickListId = 1234

What you've got right
The AST / intermediate-representation idea is the correct architectural instinct, and it's worth being clear about why. The reason word-by-word Seq2Seq SQL generation produces broken queries isn't a model-capacity problem — it's that an unconstrained decoder can emit any token at any position, including ones that make the output ungrammatical. Generating a small typed tree and compiling it deterministically removes that entire failure class. You also get parameterized queries for free (no SQL injection), schema validation at compile time, and the ability to expand one node into arbitrarily complex SQL. Keep all of this. The symbolic compiler is the strongest piece of your design.

The part that's miscategorized
Your three hard problems aren't actually the same kind of problem, and only one of them is a learning problem:

The cryptic columns (PickListId → "OBD Number") and the business macros (MPY → SMU IN ('Protective Coating','Marine') AND NatureOfTransaction = 'Sales') are knowledge, not patterns to be learned. There is a finite, enumerable set of them, they're defined by domain experts, and they change when the business changes. You do not want a neural network to infer that MPY means Marine + Protective Coating — you want a human to write that down in a config file the model can't override. A learned mapping here is strictly worse: it's unauditable, it'll occasionally hallucinate an expansion, and you can't fix a wrong macro without retraining. A curated dictionary (YAML/JSON) is correct by construction, instantly editable, and trustworthy to the business users who have to stake decisions on the output. This is the heart of your problem and it's solved by knowledge engineering, not deep learning.

The only genuine learning problem is the fuzzy front end: turning free-form English ("show me MPY sales for OBD 1234", "what were marine+protective coating numbers for picklist 1234", etc.) into the right macros and slot values. That's where a model earns its keep.

"Local" does not mean "from scratch"
This is the assumption I'd most push back on. Your no-cloud constraint rules out OpenAI's API — it does not rule out neural networks, and it definitely doesn't require building one by hand. llama.cpp, Ollama, and vLLM all run capable models fully offline, on-prem, with zero packets leaving the network. A quantized 7B coder model (Qwen2.5-Coder, etc.) runs on a single GPU.

And critically: the modern, off-the-shelf version of your "emit a clean AST instead of word-by-word SQL" idea already exists. It's called grammar-constrained decoding (GBNF in llama.cpp, Outlines/XGrammar elsewhere). You write a grammar describing your IR, and the decoder is mathematically forbidden from emitting any token that would violate it. The model literally cannot produce a malformed tree. That's the exact guarantee you were going to spend months building a custom decoder to get — available as a config file on top of a model someone else trained.

The architecture I'd actually build
Three layers, and notice the neural net is the smallest, most replaceable one:

A resolver layer — the curated dictionary. Synonyms ("OBD Number" → PickListId), macros (MPY → an IR subtree), value normalizations. Plain config, owned by domain experts, version-controlled.

A parser layer — turns English into your IR. Start with rules + fuzzy matching against the resolver; if your query space is narrow and templated, you may not need a model at all. Where free language genuinely varies, put a small local LLM here with a grammar that only emits valid IR. You can fine-tune it on synthetic (English, IR) pairs auto-generated by paraphrasing entries from the resolver — that's where ML is actually feasible locally, since you can manufacture the training data.

A compiler layer — your idea, kept whole. IR tree → validated, parameterized SQL.

The win is that each layer is independently testable and the expensive uncertain part (the model) sits behind a deterministic shell on both sides, so its mistakes are bounded.
